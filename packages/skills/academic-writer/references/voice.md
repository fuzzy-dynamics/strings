# Voice

## Sentences

Small and direct. Subject-verb-object. Active voice.

Bad: "It is the case that the spectral gap, in particular when small, leads to long evolution times being required by the adiabatic theorem."

Good: "The adiabatic theorem requires evolution time inversely proportional to the squared minimum spectral gap."

Cut every word that does not carry weight. *Very*, *quite*, *rather*, *somewhat*, *fairly* almost never carry weight. *Actually*, *basically*, *essentially* are filler.

Prefer concrete subjects. "The Hamiltonian" beats "the system being studied." "Equation (3)" beats "the above expression." "The minimum gap" beats "this quantity."

Use the present tense for mathematical statements. "The eigenvalues form a discrete set" beats "the eigenvalues will form a discrete set."

## Paragraphs

One job per paragraph. The first sentence states the job. The rest does it.

No micro-paragraphs of one or two sentences. If a paragraph is that short, it belongs to the one before, the one after, or it is filler. Find the case.

No paragraphs longer than ten sentences. If one is, it has more than one job.

Paragraphs end on the result of their work, not a teaser for the next paragraph. Do not write "we now turn to" or "in the next section."

Avoid stranded transitions. *Furthermore*, *moreover*, *thus*, *therefore*, *indeed* at the start of a paragraph almost always signal that the connection to the previous paragraph is unstated. State it.

## Voice characteristics

The reader is a peer, not a student. No condescension, no over-explaining basic linear algebra, no apologies for the difficulty.

The reader is also not the author at the moment of insight. Build from what they know to what they do not. Tension before resolution.

Honesty over cleverness. State limitations directly. State what is open. State what was tried and did not work, when relevant.

No metacommentary. Do not narrate the writing. *In this section we will*, *as we shall see*, *the rest of this chapter is organized as follows* are all metacommentary. Do the thing instead of describing it.

## Length and density

Density is the goal, not brevity. A long paragraph that earns every sentence is better than a short paragraph that hedges.

But hedging and length are the LLM defaults. The first draft is almost always too long and under-dense. Cut on every revision pass.

A useful test: read the section out loud. Sentences you stumble over are usually too long, too nested, or carry no new information.
